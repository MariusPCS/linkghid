The best chainsaw isn't the one with the most power — it's the one that matches the wood you cut and how often you cut it. Before you look at models, it helps to understand the three main types and where each one shines.

## The three types of chainsaw

### Petrol chainsaw

The most powerful option and the standard for heavy cutting, hardwood, and working far from a power outlet.

**Pros**

- High power, suited to thick trunks and dense hardwood
- Completely cordless — use it anywhere
- Long runtime: refuel and keep going
- Rugged professional ranges built for heavy use

**Cons**

- Heavier and higher vibration — more fatigue on long sessions
- Loud, with exhaust fumes
- More maintenance: fuel/oil mix, spark plug, air filter
- Fussier starting, especially in cold weather

### Corded electric chainsaw

The practical choice for light jobs around the yard, close to a power source.

**Pros**

- Light, quiet and low vibration
- Minimal maintenance, no fuel mixing
- Instant start at the press of a button
- Usually the most affordable option

**Cons**

- Tethered to a socket and extension cord — limited range
- Less power for thick trunks
- Awkward in large gardens or off-grid spots

### Battery chainsaw

The modern compromise between freedom and convenience, ideal for light-to-medium cutting.

**Pros**

- Quiet, fume-free and low maintenance
- Portable, instant start, easy to handle
- Great for branches, garden clean-up and occasional firewood

**Cons**

- Runtime is limited by the battery
- Less power than a comparable petrol saw
- Battery cost and recharge time matter

## What to look for

- **Bar (guide) length:** size it to the wood diameter. Branches and thin wood need 30–35 cm; firewood, 35–40 cm; long bars demand more power and experience.
- **Power source = use case:** small yard → electric; mobile, medium work → battery; heavy, frequent cutting → petrol.
- **Safety features:** look for a chain brake, a low-kickback chain, and an anti-vibration system.
- **Weight and ergonomics:** a saw you hold comfortably is one you control better and tire with less.
- **Easy maintenance:** tool-less chain tensioning and automatic bar oiling make the job far simpler.
- **Brand and service:** established brands like Stihl, Husqvarna, Makita, Bosch or Einhell have spare parts and replacement chains that are easy to find — a real long-term factor.

## Recommendations by scenario

- **Small yard, branches, pruning:** corded electric or battery chainsaw.
- **Occasional firewood:** a small/medium petrol saw or a powerful battery model.
- **Heavy cutting, forestry, hardwood:** a professional-grade petrol chainsaw.

## Safety (non-negotiable)

- Wear protective gear: helmet with visor, ear defenders, gloves, cut-resistant trousers and boots.
- Hold the saw with both hands and never cut above shoulder height.
- Watch for kickback: don't touch the wood with the bar tip.
- Keep the chain sharp and correctly tensioned — a dull chain is dangerous and inefficient.

## FAQ

**Electric or petrol for home use?** For a small yard and occasional jobs, an electric or battery chainsaw is enough and more convenient. For serious firewood or hardwood, petrol is still king.

**What bar length for firewood?** Around 35–40 cm covers most household logs; for large diameters you'll need a long bar and the power to match.

**How often should I sharpen the chain?** Whenever the sawdust turns to fine powder instead of chips, or when you have to force the bar into the wood.
