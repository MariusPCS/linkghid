Hypoallergenic (HA) formulas worry many parents, especially when a baby has colic, rashes or other uncomfortable symptoms. Below we explain, briefly and neutrally, what they are and why the decision must be made with a doctor — not at home, based on symptoms.

## What a hypoallergenic (HA) formula is

In a hypoallergenic formula, the milk proteins are "hydrolyzed" (broken down) into smaller fragments, so they are less likely to trigger an allergic reaction. Note: an "HA" label on an ordinary product is not the same thing as a special formula for a confirmed allergy.

## The main types

- **Partially hydrolyzed (pHF):** partially broken-down proteins. Sometimes used for digestive comfort, but **not** a treatment for a confirmed allergy.
- **Extensively hydrolyzed (eHF):** proteins broken down further; commonly used, under medical guidance, for cow's-milk protein allergy (CMPA).
- **Amino-acid based (AAF):** no intact proteins; for severe cases, only on a doctor's recommendation.

Which one is appropriate depends on the diagnosis and is decided by a doctor.

## When it comes up

Special formulas become a topic when there is a **suspected cow's-milk protein allergy**. The diagnosis is made by a doctor — many common symptoms (colic, spit-up, irregular stools) have other, more ordinary causes. That's why you should never switch formula on your own.

## What changes at 6 months

Around 6 months, **introducing solids** begins: solid foods are added gradually, alongside milk (breast milk or formula). At this age, milk remains the main source of nutrition. If there is a known allergy, new foods are introduced carefully and, ideally, with a doctor's advice.

## Signs to see a doctor

- Persistent skin rashes or hives
- Repeated vomiting or blood in the stool
- Poor weight gain
- **Difficulty breathing or swelling — this is an emergency, call your local emergency number immediately**

## The most important thing

Breastfeeding remains the first recommendation where possible. Don't use an "HA" formula as a treatment without a diagnosis, and don't change formula on your own. For any decision about feeding your baby, talk to your pediatrician or a pediatric allergist.
